1. Big Ideas from Chapter 3 – Communicate with Confidence

Chapter 3 reminds us that business lives and breathes through communication. Good communicators become trusted professionals and leaders because they can explain ideas clearly, solve problems, and work well with others.

1.1 Communicating in Writing

Think before you write

  • What is my main message?
  • Does this need to be in writing, or is a quick conversation better?
  • Who is my audience, and what do they already know?

Think while you write

  • Be clear: Use simple words and short sentences.
  • Be considerate: Think about how your message will sound to the reader.
  • Be correct: Check spelling, grammar, and facts.
  • Be logical: Use transitions so ideas flow smoothly.

After you write, revise

  • Delete extra words that don’t add value.
  • Add any missing details (who, what, when, where, how).
  • Check tone – is it respectful and professional?
  • When it matters, have someone else read it.

1.2 Word Choice in Business

Your word choice shows your professionalism. Chapter 3 suggests:

  • Use clear, everyday language your audience can understand.
  • Avoid slang, filler words, ALL CAPS, and excessive emojis.
  • Use jargon or technical terms only when the audience knows them.
  • Be careful with humor or “jokes” in writing – they don’t always translate well.

1.3 Speaking with Confidence

  • Prepare: Know your topic and organize your key points.
  • Audience first: What do they need from you?
  • Use the simple formula:
    • Tell them what you’ll tell them.
    • Tell them.
    • Tell them what you told them.
  • Keep it short and focused—people stop listening after 15–20 minutes.
  • Make eye contact, speak slowly, and pause instead of saying “um.”

1.4 Visual Communication

  • Use visuals (slides, charts, graphs) to support your message, not distract from it.
  • Limit text on slides – focus on key points.
  • Don’t talk while people are busy reading a dense slide.
  • Only use visuals that have a clear purpose.

3. Quick Communication Checklists

3.1 Writing Checklist (Email, Memo, Message)

  • Clear subject line that matches the content.
  • Opening sentence explains the purpose.
  • Short paragraphs and simple sentences.
  • Includes who, what, when, where, and how.
  • Polite tone (please, thank you, respectful language).
  • No slang, ALL CAPS, or unnecessary emojis.
  • Spell-check and proofread before sending.

3.2 Speaking Checklist (Meetings & Presentations)

  • Know your main points (don’t try to memorize every word).
  • Use the formula: intro → main points → recap.
  • Talk slowly, pause, and avoid filler words (“um,” “like”).
  • Make eye contact with different people in the room.
  • Stand or sit upright; don’t fidget with notes or objects.
  • Use examples or short stories to make ideas real.
  • Finish with a clear conclusion or next step.

3.3 Visuals Checklist (Slides, Charts, Handouts)

  • Each visual has a clear purpose (not just “because it looks cool”).
  • Limited text – only key points on each slide.
  • Fonts are large enough for everyone to read.
  • Colors are easy to see (no neon on neon).
  • You face the audience, not the screen.
  • You don’t read the slide word-for-word.
  • You could still explain your message without the visuals if needed.

4. Reflection & Discussion Questions

Use these questions for class discussion, small groups, or your own reflection:

  1. Think of a time when miscommunication caused a problem at work or school. What went wrong, and how could clearer writing or speaking have helped?
  2. Which part of communication is hardest for you right now: writing, speaking, or using visuals? Why?
  3. Look back at Scene 1. If you were the manager, how would you rewrite the original email and instructions?
  4. How can you apply the “think before, while, and after you write” idea in your next real email?
  5. What’s one change you can make this week to sound more confident when you speak?

5. Quick Summary of Chapter 3

  • Good communication is essential for success in business.
  • Strong writing comes from planning, clarity, and careful revision.
  • Your word choice and tone shape how people see you professionally.
  • Confident speaking is built on preparation, structure, and practice.
  • Visual aids should support your message, not replace it or distract from it.

When you combine these skills, you don’t just “get your point across” — you show that you’re reliable, professional, and ready for more responsibility.
